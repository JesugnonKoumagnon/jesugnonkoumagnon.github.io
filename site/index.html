About

Why this work.

I grew up in Benin, in a region where a bad rainy season was never just weather — it decided whether a harvest failed, whether a clinic had patients it couldn't treat, whether a family stayed put or moved on. That link between environmental shock and human vulnerability is what pulled me into statistics, and later into economics: not as an abstraction, but as a way to actually measure who gets left behind, and why.

My work since has carried that thread across borders — household surveys in Benin, child poverty data in Côte d'Ivoire, conflict and climate data around Lake Chad, and now humanitarian assessments in Sudan. What ties it together is a belief that good evidence, built carefully and communicated clearly, can change what governments and agencies choose to do next.

Areas of inquiry

Three questions I keep returning to.

Climate & Conflict

Does a drying climate fuel violence?

Panel econometrics linking temperature, rainfall, and vegetation anomalies to conflict intensity — tested at district and grid-cell level across the Lake Chad basin (Cameroon, Chad, Niger, Nigeria).

Social Protection

Who do safety-net programs actually reach?

Mapping the gap between where poverty is worst and where social assistance is delivered — using Multidimensional Poverty Index and national social register data.

Child Poverty

How do we make deprivation visible to policymakers?

Survey analysis and interactive dashboards translating household-level deprivation data into tools governments and UN agencies can act on.

Methods

Causal inference under messy, real-world data.

Fixed-effects panels, spatial lag models, RCT design, GIS aggregation, and machine learning applied to survey and satellite data across multi-country settings.
